# Research Plan v1

## Research Question

How well do large language models perform on mathematical reasoning tasks, and what are their failure modes?

## Hypothesis

LLMs achieve high accuracy on routine math problems but struggle with multi-step reasoning and novel problem structures. Specifically, we expect models like GPT-4 to perform well on single-step arithmetic and algebraic problems from GSM8K (>85% accuracy) but exhibit significant degradation on problems requiring 3 or more reasoning steps from the MATH dataset (<50% accuracy).

## Motivation

Mathematical reasoning is a core proxy for general intelligence. As LLMs are increasingly deployed in educational, scientific, and financial settings, understanding the boundary between reliable computation and systematic failure is critical. Current benchmarks report aggregate accuracy but do not isolate which reasoning steps break down, making it difficult to target improvements. This research aims to produce a granular failure-mode taxonomy that can guide both prompt-engineering strategies and model architecture changes.

## Methodology

We will evaluate a suite of frontier and open-weight LLMs on two established math benchmarks, segment problems by reasoning depth, and perform error analysis on failures. The approach consists of:

1. **Model selection**: Evaluate GPT-4, Claude 3.5 Sonnet, Llama-3-70B, and Mistral-7B
2. **Benchmark curation**: Use GSM8K (grade-school math) and MATH (competition-level) with stratified difficulty
3. **Prompting strategies**: Compare zero-shot, few-shot, and [[concept-chain-of-thought]] prompting
4. **Error taxonomy**: Classify each failure into categories (arithmetic error, reasoning gap, misinterpretation, hallucination)
5. **Statistical analysis**: Report accuracy by depth, difficulty, and model; compute inter-annotator agreement on error labels

## Experiment Design (outline)

- **E1**: Baseline evaluation of all models on GSM8K and MATH with standard prompting
- **E2**: Ablation over prompting strategies (zero-shot vs few-shot vs CoT)
- **E3**: Error taxonomy annotation and analysis on E1 failures

## Expected Outcomes

- **Confirming hypothesis**: Models show >30 percentage point drop between single-step and multi-step problems; error taxonomy reveals reasoning gaps as dominant failure mode
- **Rejecting hypothesis**: Models perform uniformly across step counts, suggesting the challenge is in knowledge retrieval rather than reasoning depth
- **Actionable artifact**: A published error taxonomy dataset and per-model breakdown table
